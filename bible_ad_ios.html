What is Fixed Income?

By Viraj Yale

April 28, 2023 15 min read

Fixed income refers to investment securities that pay a fixed interest or dividend to investors before their maturity date. At maturity, investors receive their invested principal. Government and corporate bonds are the most common types of fixed income products. Unlike stocks or variable income securities that may not provide cash flows to investors, in which payments may vary based on certain underlying indicators (such as short-term interest rates), fixed income securities have predetermined and constant payments throughout their duration. In addition to directly purchasing fixed income securities, investors can also invest in various fixed income Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) and mutual funds.

Learn about fixed income

Fixed income refers to the issuance of debt securities by corporations and governments to raise funds for their day-to-day operations and large-scale projects. For investors, fixed income instruments offer a fixed rate of return in exchange for lending their capital. Upon maturity, investors receive the repayment of their initial investment, known as the principal. For example, a company may issue a bond with a 5% coupon rate and a face value of $1,000, maturing in five years. An investor purchases the bond for $1,000 and will be repaid at the end of the five-year period. During these five years, the company pays interest, known as coupon payments, based on the 5% annual interest rate. Hence, the investor receives $50 annually over the five-year period. At the end of the five years, the investor receives the initial investment of $1,000 upon maturity. Investors can also find fixed income investments that pay coupons monthly, quarterly, or semi-annually. Fixed income securities are recommended for conservative investors seeking portfolio diversification. The percentage of a portfolio dedicated to fixed income depends on an investor's risk tolerance. There is also an opportunity to diversify portfolios by combining fixed income products with equities, creating a portfolio that may consist of 50% fixed income and 50% stocks. Examples of fixed income products include government bonds and notes, municipal bonds, corporate bonds, and certificates of deposit (CDs). Bonds are traded over-the-counter (OTC) in the bond market and secondary markets.

Types of fixed income products

As mentioned earlier, the most common examples of fixed income securities are government or corporate bonds. The most common government securities are issued by the U.S. government and are typically referred to as Treasury securities. Non-U.S. governments and corporations also offer fixed income securities. Here are the most common types of fixed income products: Treasury Bills (T-bills): Short-term fixed income securities with maturities of one year or less that do not pay coupon interest. Investors purchase bills at a price below face value and earn the difference at maturity. Treasury Notes (T-notes): Fixed income securities with maturities ranging from 2 to 10 years that pay a fixed interest rate and are sold in multiples of $100. Investors receive the repayment of principal at the end of the term along with semi-annual interest payments until maturity. Treasury Bonds (T-bonds): Similar to T-notes but with longer maturities of 20 or 30 years. Treasury bonds can be purchased in multiples of $100. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS): Bonds that protect investors against inflation. The principal of TIPS bonds adjusts with inflation and deflation. Municipal Bonds: Similar to Treasuries, but issued and supported by state, city, or county governments to raise funds for local expenditures. Municipal bonds may also offer tax advantages to investors. Corporate Bonds: Various types of bonds issued by corporations, with the issuance price and interest rate largely dependent on the company's financial stability and creditworthiness. Higher-rated bonds typically pay lower coupon rates. Junk Bonds - also known as high-yield bonds - are corporate bonds that pay higher yields due to higher default risk. Default refers to a company's failure to repay the principal and interest on its bonds or debt securities. Certificates of Deposit (CDs): Fixed income instruments offered by financial institutions with maturities of less than five years. They offer higher interest rates than typical savings accounts and are protected by the FDIC or the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA). These are some of the common types of fixed income products available to investors.

How to Invest in Fixed Income

Investors looking to add fixed income securities to their investment portfolio have various options. Nowadays, most brokers provide channels for clients to directly access a range of bond markets, from government bonds to corporate bonds and municipal bonds. For those who do not wish to select individual bonds, fixed income mutual funds (bond funds) offer exposure to a variety of bonds and debt instruments. These funds allow investors to gain income streams through professionally managed portfolios. Fixed income ETFs operate similarly to mutual funds but may be more accessible and cost-effective for individual investors. These ETFs may target specific credit ratings, durations, or other factors. ETFs also incur professional management fees. Fixed income investments are typically considered conservative strategies, where returns come from low-risk securities that pay predictable interest. Due to the lower risk, coupon payments are typically lower as well. Building a fixed income investment portfolio may involve investing in bonds, bond mutual funds, and certificates of deposit (CDs). One such strategy utilizing fixed income products is called a ladder strategy. A ladder strategy provides stable interest income by investing in a series of short-term bonds. As bonds mature, the portfolio manager reinvests the returned principal into new short-term bonds, expanding the ladder. This approach allows investors to have access to ready funds and avoid losses due to rising market interest rates. For example, a $60,000 investment can be divided into one-year, two-year, and three-year bonds. The $60,000 principal is divided into three equal parts, with $20,000 invested in each of the three bonds. When the one-year bond matures, the $20,000 principal is rolled into the three-year bond that originally had two years remaining. When the second bond matures, the funds are rolled into a bond, extending the ladder for another year. Through this approach, investors can earn stable interest income returns and take advantage of any higher rates available.

Advantages of Fixed Income

Income Generation: Fixed income investments provide investors with a stable income stream throughout the life cycle of the bonds or debt instruments. At the same time, they serve as a vital capital or funding channel for the issuer. The stable income allows investors to plan their expenses, making fixed income products popular in retirement investment portfolios. Relative Stability: The interest payments from fixed income products can also help stabilize the risk-return profile of investors' portfolios, specifically market risk. For investors holding stocks, prices can be volatile, leading to significant gains or losses. The stable interest payments from fixed income securities partially offset the losses that may arise from stock price declines. Therefore, these secure investments help diversify the risk in an investment portfolio. Security: Furthermore, fixed income investments in the form of Treasury bonds (T-bonds) are backed by the support of the U.S. government. While corporate bonds are not insured, they are supported by the financial viability of the underlying companies. Bondholders have higher claim on company assets in the event of bankruptcy or liquidation compared to common stock shareholders. Additionally, bond investments held by brokerage firms are supported by the Securities Investor Protection Corporation (SIPC), providing insurance coverage of up to $500,000 for cash and securities held by the firm. Fixed income CDs are protected by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC), offering up to $250,000 per person in coverage.

Risks Associated with Fixed Income

While fixed income products offer many benefits like any other investment, investors should be aware of certain risks before making a purchase. Credit and Default Risk: As mentioned earlier, Treasury bonds and Certificates of Deposit (CDs) are backed by the government and FDIC protection, respectively. Corporate bonds, while not as secure, still rank higher in repayment priority compared to shareholders. When choosing investments, it's important to review the credit ratings of bonds and the issuing companies. Bonds with ratings below BBB are considered lower quality and fall into the category of junk bonds. Company-specific credit risk can have varying effects on the valuation of fixed income instruments before maturity. If a company faces financial distress, the prices of its bonds in the secondary market may decline. If an investor attempts to sell bonds of a troubled company, the selling price may be below the face value or even face difficulty finding buyers due to lack of demand. Bond prices can fluctuate up or down during the life of the bond. If an investor holds the bond until maturity, price fluctuations are not of concern since the investor will receive the bond's face value at maturity. However, if bondholders sell the bond before maturity through a broker or financial institution, they will receive the current market price at the time of sale. Depending on the issuing company, coupon rates, and prevailing market rates, the selling price may result in investment gains or losses. Interest Rate Risk: Fixed income investors may face interest rate risk. This risk occurs when market rates rise, and the rates paid by bonds lag behind. In such a scenario, bonds will depreciate in the secondary bond market. Additionally, investors' capital is tied up in the investment, and they are unable to put it towards earning higher income without incurring initial losses. For example, if an investor purchases a two-year bond with a 2.5% annual interest rate, and two-year bond rates soar to 5%, the investor is locked in at 2.5%. Regardless of whether rates are favorable or not, investors holding fixed income products will receive a fixed rate, irrespective of how market rates change. Inflation Risk: Inflation risk is also a threat to fixed income investors. The rate at which prices increase in the economy is known as inflation. If prices rise or inflation intensifies, it erodes the returns of fixed income securities. For instance, if a fixed rate debt security pays a return of 2%, and the inflation rate rises to 1.5%, investors will experience a loss, with a real return of only 0.5%.

Fixed Income Analysis: Things to Consider

When deciding which financial products to invest in, investors conduct fixed-income analysis. The following techniques are used to evaluate which investments are most suitable based on the investor's risk preference and expected returns. Fixed-income analysis typically begins with risk assessment. All investments involve a trade-off between risk and return; all else being equal, higher-risk investments should offer higher returns. Therefore, fixed-income analysis evaluates whether investors are comfortable with the level of risk they are taking and assesses whether the risk level aligns with the expected returns of fixed-income securities. For fixed-income securities, risk is associated with the creditworthiness of the issuing company, the duration of the fixed-income security, and the industry in which the company operates. For example, government bonds, often associated with lower returns, are considered safer investments due to lower default risk. On the other hand, corporate bonds, especially those issued by companies with cash flow issues, may carry higher risk. The repayment structure of the investment is also considered. Some fixed-income securities offer regular payments, allowing investors to recoup their capital over the investment period. This reduces risk because not all capital is tied up until the potential maturity of a long-term bond. Finally, different fixed-income securities have various features that make them more or less appealing. Some may be callable, allowing the issuer to repay the entire bond before maturity. Others may have the option to convert the fixed-income security into common stock. Considering which features are important to you is crucial because each advantageous term may lower the yield.

the bottom line

Fixed income refers to debt investments that pay a fixed interest rate and return the borrowed principal at maturity. This includes different types of bonds and certificates of deposit. As an asset class, fixed income is typically considered more conservative and less volatile than stocks. A well-diversified investment portfolio should have some allocation to fixed income, and as one's time horizon shortens (e.g., approaching retirement), the allocation to fixed income tends to increase.
